Background Information on School Choice

School choice is the idea that families should have a say in where their children attend school. The two most widely discussed types of school choice in the United States are vouchers and charter schools.

Charter schools are public schools that are managed separately from traditional public schools. They are accountable for pursuing the goals established in the schools' charter. The majority (68%) of charter schools are operated independently (i.e. as a single school rather than a member of a network), with the remaining schools operating in various networks including "Charter Management Organizations" like the Knowledge is Power Program (KIPP) and Uncommon Schools. Sources of contention include whether charter schools fail to address issues of school segregation, inflate their scores by "skimming" the best students or teaching to the standardized tests, if (and where) they outperform traditional public schools, and what explains the differences in states' charter laws.

Vouchers are government funds that families can use toward the cost of private schools. Whereas advocates of vouchers approve of the increased choice afforded to families, criticism centers on the use of vouchers at religious schools and the effectiveness of voucher programs.

Charters and Vouchers, Increasingly, Are a Political Reality.

The nation's first charter school was founded in 1992. By the 2015-2016 academic year, charter schools constituted a full 7% of the nation's public schools. The distribution of these schools is highly uneven. For example, 49.1% of the D.C. public schools and 24.2% of Arizona schools were charter schools. At the other end of the distribution are Alabama, Montana, Nebraska, South Dakota, Vermont and West Virginia, none of which had charter schools in the year under consideration.

Vouchers have a smaller market share. EdChoice, an organization that promotes school choice, reported in June of 2018 that there were just over 190,000 students receiving vouchers nationwide.

Charter Schools Historically a Bipartisan Cause among Party Leaders

President Trump's Secretary of Education, Betsy DeVos, has made school choice a central focus of her tenure. This--and the vocal charter school criticisms made by teachers' unions and left-leaning opponents of charter schools and vouchers--have given the impression that school choice is a squarely conservative cause.

Yet the charter school movement has antecedents in both parties. President Clinton advanced the then nascent charter school movement--so much so that the National Alliance for Public Charter Schools gave him a lifetime achievement award in 2011. President Obama's educational reforms also encompassed charter schools. His signature "Race to the Top" program, which launched in 2009 and ultimately awarded more than $4 billion of federal education funding to states, listed among the scoring and selection criteria that states should not have laws against charter schools.

During the 2016 election, the Democratic Party maintained a cautiously supportive stance toward non-profit charter schools, provided that they not undermine traditional public schools or underserve students of any particular demographic groups:

Democrats are committed to providing parents with high-quality public school options and expanding these options for low-income youth. We support democratically governed, great neighborhood public schools and high-quality public charter schools, and we will help them disseminate best practices to other school leaders and educators. Democrats oppose for-profit charter schools focused on making a profit off of public resources. We believe that high-quality public charter schools should provide options for parents, but should not replace or destabilize traditional public schools. Charter schools must reflect their communities, and thus must accept and retain proportionate numbers of students of color, students with disabilities and English Language Learners in relation to their neighborhood public schools. We support increased transparency and accountability for all charter schools.

Source: DNC Platform, 2016.

Notably, the 2016 party platform did not mention school vouchers.

Public Opinion Mixed on School Choice

Partisan Divides in Public Support for Charter Schools, But Not for Vouchers

A 2018 survey from Education Next asked respondents whether they "support or oppose the formation of charter schools" and if they would support a proposal "that would give low-income families with children in public schools a voucher" that could be used toward private schools.

Opinions did not cleave as neatly to party lines when it came to vouchers. Surprisingly, Democrat support for vouchers targeting low-income families was even higher than Republican support, at 39% and 47% respectively (Responses varried slightly based on the exact phrasing; see p.11 of the Education Next report). The trend flipped when Education Next asked about universal--as opposed to targeted--vouchers: A full 64% of Republicans but only 47% of Democrats said they would support universal--as opposed to targeted--vouchers (See p.10). This suggests that Republican's responses are very sensitive to the wording of the question and that Republican support for vouchers is largely contingent on who would be eligible.

A more interesting difference in support for vouchers actually relates to ethnic groups. The majority of surveyed Hispanics and African Americans supported vouchers. Charter schools were slightly less popular among members of these demographic groups. Conversely, white respondents had a relative preference for charters over vouchers.

The already tepid charter school support among Democratic voters seems to be trending downward slightly. Although opinion among surveyed Democrats has been relatively stable since 2013, public opinion may shift. The unexpectedly heated debate in Massachusetts over a 2016 proposal to increase the charter cap, and the nationwide attention to Betsy DeVos' appointment to be Secretary of Education both show how quickly school choice can flare up as a headline-grabbing issue. The current administration's promotion of school vouchers and charter schools may also spark a realignment of the political landscape, according to commentators who anticipate diminishing liberal support.

Joe Biden voted against school vouchers in D.C.in 1997, according to OnTheIssues.org. More recently, Biden supported President Obama's education agenda, which included incentives for states to allow charter schoools.
Bloomberg has worked to expand school choice in New York City.
Booker supports school choice but opposed the appointment of Secretary DeVos.
Ohio Senator Sherrod Brown has voted against vouchers and in 2015 introduced the Charter School Accountability Act, which called for greater transparency and accountability in Charter Schools.
Montana Governor Steve Bullock has largely steered clear of school choice. In 2015 he declined to sign a tax-credit scholarship bill, which nonetheless became law. He has at times expressed skepticism about charter schools, which are almost nonexistant in Montana. A spokesperson of Bullock said in 2017 that the legislature should not be "playing last-minute politics with efforts to divert public funds away from public schools," a remark obliquely critical of school choice programs.
Pete Buttigieg, the Mayor of South Bend, Indiana, has not taken a clear stance on charter schools and voucher programs.
Julian Castro, who served as the United States Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, has focused his proposed education reforms on universal pre-school. Castro has demonstrated a willingness to work with public, charter, and private schools as part of the Pre-K 4 SA (San Antonio) initiative.
Hillary Clinton, previously a supporter of charter schools, struck a slightly more critical tone in the lead up to the 2016 election when she stated that charters “don’t take the hardest-to-teach kids, or, if they do, they don’t keep them.” This is a notable departure from the pro-charter policies enacted by President Bill Clinton.
Governor Cuomo is a supporter of charter schools. In 2017 he proposed eliminating the cap on charter schools. In the lead up to the 2018 election he received $130,000 from charter school supporters.
Former U.S. Representative John Delaney has placed a strong emphasis on education and supports charter schools.
U.S. Representative Tulsi Gabbard does not list education in the vision outlined on her website. Stay tuned for updates.
Senator Kirsten Gillibrand (D-N.Y.) has positioned herself as an opponent of Betsy DeVos, whom she called the "wrong choice." She seems to prioritize traditional public schools.
Senator Kamala Harris (D-CA) has sharply criticised proposals of arming educators. During her years in the Senate she has not focused extensively on education, according to Education Week
Colorado Governor John Hickenlooper is a supporter of charter schools; his stance on vouchers is less clear. He made January 21 to 27 "Colorado School Choice Week."
In 2016 Governor Jay Inslee of Washington State neither signed nor vetoed a bill that would keep charter schools open. He apparently opposed a proposed reauthorization of the Opportunity Scholarship program in D.C., which is a type of voucehr program.
The daughter of a teacher, Senator Amy Klobuchar (D-MN) opposed the confirmation of Betsy DeVos. Previously, Klobuchar had voted against the reauthorization of a D.C. voucher program.
Former Louisiana Senator Mary seems enthusiastic about charter schools and cautious with regard to vouchers. Landrieu wrote in an op-ed that New Orleans charter schools were positivly impacting the city's education. Although she has praised educational reforms initiated by Governor Bobby Jindall, she said "I hope the governor does not pull the rug out from under some of the public schools by relying too much on vouchers."
Senator Jeff Merkley (D-OR) has not made decisive statements on his website about vouchers and charter schools, but seems critical of the Trump administration’s approach. During an appropriations hearing in 2017, Merkley cut Secretary DeVos no slack, questioning her on potential discrimination in charter and private schools.
Senator Richard Ojeda (D-WV) supported the 2018 teacher strike in his state. Some of the recent teacher strikes nationwide have been calling for caps on charter schools. It seems plausible that a candidate who aligns himself with striking teachers or teachers' unions will be unenthusiastic about charters.
Beto O’Rourke opposes private school vouchers. His wife is a charter school administrator.
A critic of what he once referred to as "private charter schools," Senator Sanders opposes for-profit charters. Bernie Sanders criticized a proposed charter cap increase in Massachusetts on the grounds that it would divert resources away from public schools.
Billionaire philanthropist Tom Steyer is known largely for his environmentalism, but his website identifies education as one of the 5 Rights he hopes to fight for. His education agenda will be worth watching as 2020 approaches.

Senator Warren opposed a proposed charter cap increase in Massachusetts. She explained her position by saying:
