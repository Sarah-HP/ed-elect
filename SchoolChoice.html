School Choice

The two most widely discussed types of school choice in the United States are vouchers and charter schools. The nation's first charter school was founded in 1992. By the 2015-2016 academic year, charter schools constituted a full 7% of the nation's public schools. The distribution of these schools is highly uneven. For example, 49.1% of the D.C. public schools and 24.2% of Arizona schools were charter schools. At the other end of the distribution are Alabama, Montana, Nebraska, South Dakota, Vermont, West Virginia, none of which had charter schools in the year under consideration.

As of 2016, the Democratic National Committee expressed general support for charter schools, though the party platform criticized for-profit charters and addressed concerns that charter schools are underrepresenting minority students:

Democrats are committed to providing parents with high-quality public school options and expanding these options for low-income youth. We support democratically governed, great neighborhood public schools and high-quality public charter schools, and we will help them disseminate best practices to other school leaders and educators. Democrats oppose for-profit charter schools focused on making a profit off of public resources. We believe that high-quality public charter schools should provide options for parents, but should not replace or destabilize traditional public schools. Charter schools must reflect their communities, and thus must accept and retain proportionate numbers of students of color, students with disabilities and English Language Learners in relation to their neighborhood public schools. We support increased transparency and accountability for all charter schools.

Source: DNC Platform, 2016.

Since the appointment of Secretary of Education Betsy DeVos, vouchers as a form of school choice have taken on even greater salience. Questions of education will undoubtedly loom large in the 2020 presidential election.

A recent poll of opinion data reveals that Democrats have lukework support toward charrter schools and school vouchers.

School choice enjoys somewhat more popularity among the Republican party. Interestingly enough, teachers have been the least enthusiastic about charter and voucher programs. This suggests that opinions on educational reform do not always cleave neatly to party lines.

A 2018 poll from Education Next used the following questions to gauge the latest trends in public opinion.

1. As you may know, many states permit the formation of charter schools, which are publicly funded but are not managed by the local school board. These schools are expected to meet promised objectives, but are exempt from many state regulations. Do you support or oppose the formation of charter schools?

2. A proposal has been made that would give low-income families with children in public schools a voucher allowing them to enroll their children in attend[sic] private schools instead, with government helping to pay the tuition. Would you support or oppose the proposal?

Source: Education Next, Program on Education Policy and Governance -- Survey 2018
